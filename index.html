Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Does the spatial concentration of corporate property acquisitions correlate with the demographic exclusion of Black and Hispanic communities, or is capital targeting specific "transitioning" neighborhoods?

    Prior to beginning the visual analysis, I intend to map the geographic footprint of corporate landlords to determine whether they are disproportionately acquiring properties in Black, Hispanic, and Asian communities. This motivation stems from the "Homes for All Massachusetts" report, which starkly illustrates the severe racial inequities in eviction filings across municipalities like Boston and Brockton. The report demonstrates that evictions are not random occurrences but highly targeted spatial phenomena. Furthermore, the New York Times investigation into 83 million eviction records emphasizes the sweeping, intimate devastation this crisis inflicts upon families. I aim to investigate whether corporate landlords act as the primary spatial drivers of these neighborhood-level displacements, thereby systematically reshaping the demographic and cultural fabric of the city.

  2. Are corporate investors systematically targeting and removing multi-family housing typologies—specifically Boston's historic two-family and three-family residences—from the local ownership market?

    Historically, the "triple-decker" was uniquely designed as a crucial mechanism for working-class wealth building, functioning to offer affordable, multi-generational living within a compact urban footprint. The New York Times piece discusses how the commodification of housing alters communities at a fundamental structural level. By analyzing the property types and bedroom counts associated with these real estate transactions, I seek to ascertain if the architectural "missing middle" is being monopolized. If corporate entities are dominating the acquisition of these specific structures, they are fundamentally subverting the original architectural purpose of Boston's housing stock, transforming community-sustaining residences into purely speculative financial assets.

  3. To what extent do predatory market tactics, such as rapid property flipping and intentional "warehousing," restrict the available housing supply and exacerbate neighborhood instability?

    This inquiry bridges the disciplines of urban economics and urban planning. Both the eviction data highlighted in the New York Times and the structural inequalities detailed in the "Homes for All" report point to a housing market ecosystem that prioritizes capital accumulation over residential stability. From a planning perspective, a vacant building signifies both a neighborhood blight and a failure of spatial utilization. I am motivated to examine the correlation between high corporate ownership rates and localized vacancy rates. If institutional investors are intentionally leaving buildings empty to artificially inflate neighborhood rents or facilitate future flipping, it represents a manufactured scarcity that directly fuels the ongoing eviction crisis.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

The data illustrates a significant transformation in Boston’s housing market, characterized by a steady rise in corporate ownership that culminated in 2023 when the citywide corporate-owned rate officially surpassed the owner-occupancy rate. This trend accelerated following the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, with corporate control now heavily concentrated in areas like the South Boston Waterfront and Downtown. Yet, a higher corporate ownership rates are currently found in neighborhoods with higher minority populations, and the corporate ownership rate is not positively correlated with Black and Latino communities.

Visual analysis debunked several hypotheses regarding predatory market tactics, specifically the idea that investors are monopolizing the "missing middle" or intentionally "warehousing" units. The data reveals that historic two-family and three-family residences, known as "triple-deckers," are not dominated by corporate buyers but remain largely under individual ownership. Furthermore, there is no evidence of "manufactured scarcity" through intentional vacancies, as vacancy rates do not rise significantly alongside increases in corporate ownership. These findings suggest that while corporate expansion is a reality, it has not yet overtaken the specific architectural typologies historically used for working-class wealth building.

Despite the lack of evidence for warehousing, the human toll of corporate dominance remains high, as majority-nonwhite neighborhoods face disproportionately higher eviction threats. Across Massachusetts, 43% of all eviction filings occurred in majority-nonwhite neighborhoods, even though these areas account for only 32% of the rental housing stock. Research indicates that landlords with a local stake, such as live-in owners, file significantly fewer evictions for non-payment or cause compared to absentee corporate entities. Consequently, the transition toward corporate control likely exacerbates the regional eviction crisis by replacing community-sustaining residences with speculative assets that prioritize capital accumulation over residential stability.
